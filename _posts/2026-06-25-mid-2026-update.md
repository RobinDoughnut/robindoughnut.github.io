# Life update and how to make a cheap quadcopter drone

## life update

a lot of time has passed since i last wrote anything. i think i should write some life update.

currently i am just trying to figure out if i should be getting ready for WW3, another pandemic, the AI takeover, economic collapse, or all of the above. i thought instead i could write about an interesting project i was part of during my last academic semester, spring 2026.

## how to make a cheap drone

you ever feel like nothing good was gonna happen to you?

4 years of undergrad passed by and you still struggle to write a single line of code without AI. you see people vibe coding startups you can't even fathom. every linkedin post sounds like someone accidentally invented AGI.

well nothing did get better for me and i've been having severe imposter syndrome for the last few months. you start to wonder if you somehow got accepted into the wrong degree. you've been fixing the wifi in your home by switching it off and on and somehow got into a Computer Science and Engineering program, and now you think this is just a glorified maths degree. the child in you who jailbroke an ipad and ran a sandbox attack during a war in clash of clans is dead.

nothing is normal anymore. everything and everyone is quantified and you can't just do things anymore for the sake of it. you can't just make something you're interested in. everything has to be quantified.

you can't just build a drone because you think flying machines are cool. it has to become a startup. a side hustle. a portfolio project. a LinkedIn post. a case study.

you can't just learn something because you're curious. you have to optimize it. monetize it. turn it into a roadmap. put it on your resume. measure its ROI.

every hobby has to become a business. every interest has to become a career move. every free afternoon has to become an investment in your future. we forgot that making things used to be enough. i remember making a random shitty minecraft world in creative mode, and a water-measuring project at the Dhaka Residential School science fair where we got really humbled. we built things that didn't matter.

we weren't trying to disrupt an industry. we weren't trying to get venture funding. we were bored and curious. and that was good enough.

this constant state of existential angst pushed me to try a new thing. i gave up on trying to make something cool for my cv and said fk it we ball.

i took the embedded systems course with one of my friends whom i met in a very chinese state of my life, we'll call him Z. he's a calm and quiet guy. the type of person who could stare at a broken circuit board for ten seconds and somehow know what was wrong with it. nobody knew exactly what he was doing at any given moment, but whenever he spoke, people listened.

when sir told us to form a group for the project, me and Z decided to ask this random girl who sat in the front row and took really good notes if she wanted to form a group. she looked like a serious student. also we could take her notes. she said she was still thinking about whom she wanted to form a group with, which is a polite way of saying no, and we got rejected.

we moved on. Z said he had a mutual friend from his neighborhood who seemed like a chill guy. he's known as J, and we'll call him prodigy, since he joined uni at 16 and will graduate before he turns 21. when the rest of us spent three hours debugging a typo in the Arduino IDE, J would casually mention he'd already solved the problem yesterday. that guy was smart, polite, and knew how to get shit done.

J recommended someone new to add to the group. he's known as C, and that guy was our tony stark. he was also near the end of his undergrad and had little to no life left in him. if things had gone right, this guy would've ended up at SpaceX helping Elon colonize Mars. C did not build flying suits. now he just wants to get out of NSU with a decent cgpa like the rest of us. but tony stark had ideas. an endless supply of ideas. he told us he wanted to build an autonomous drone with the cheapest hardware we could buy off the roboticsbd website. we locked in.

we were pissed off about the death of a persian leader and figured maybe building an autonomous drone could help him win the war. reader, hold that thought.

---

so the plan, as C explained it, was simple in the way that all doomed engineering plans are simple. build an autonomous drone. from scratch. with the cheapest parts roboticsbd had in stock. no pre-built flight controller you just flash and forget. we'd write the brain ourselves, line by line, so that every dumb decision the drone made would be a dumb decision we personally authored.

the parts list reads like a shopping cart from someone who has never flown anything in their life:

- an **ESP32-S3 super mini** for the brain. a microcontroller smaller than a stick of gum.
- an **MPU6050** for its inner ear, the little chip that tells it which way is up.
- two **DRV8833** boards for the muscle.
- four **coreless brushed motors**, the kind that go in toy drones and die if you look at them wrong.
- a perfboard, some 3D-printed clips, and props. red ones on the front and back, black ones on the sides, so we could tell which way the thing was facing when it inevitably tried to kill itself.

total damage: a few thousand taka. cheaper than a night out, if you have an expensive social life, which i do not.

we did it the boring correct way, one layer at a time, because Z insisted and Z is always right about these things. first we just made the ESP32 say hello to the MPU over i2c — a who-am-i check, basically asking the chip "are you there?" and waiting for it to answer. then we read the gyro and accelerometer and watched the numbers scroll past in the serial monitor like the matrix, if the matrix was extremely boring. then one motor. then four. then the mixer — the math that turns "go up" and "lean left" into four different motor speeds. then the controls: `w` to speed up, `s` to slow down, `c` to arm, `p` to stop. four letters. an entire aircraft controlled by four letters typed into an arduino window.

and then the bugs. oh, the bugs.

first, the drone thought it was always rolling. infinitely. the number that's supposed to say "how tilted am i" just climbed forever, off toward infinity, very calmly, as if the drone had quietly decided it was a helicopter blade. turned out we'd mounted the MPU upside down on the underside of the board. the chip was reading the world flipped, the angle math hit a wraparound where it can't decide between +180 and −180 degrees, and the whole thing spiraled. we didn't remount it. we just lied to it in software — flipped the axes in code so an upside-down chip believes it's right side up. a small dishonest fix for a small dishonest machine.

then we told it to pitch forward and it rolled sideways instead. spent an embarrassing number of hours hunting for a control-loop bug that did not exist, because the actual problem was that the code was written for an X-shaped drone and we'd built a +-shaped one. every motor was in the wrong place relative to what the brain believed. rewrote the mixing math, and suddenly "lean forward" actually leaned it forward. you have not known peace until a machine finally does the one thing you've been screaming at it to do for two days.

then the motors spun and the gyro readings turned to complete garbage, because brushed motors shake like a washing machine full of bricks, and the sensor was bolted to the same shaking board. fixed it by switching on the chip's most aggressive internal filter — register `0x06`, the "please ignore the chaos" setting.

and then, the final boss. every time we ran all four motors at any real throttle, the entire ESP32 would just… reboot. mid-command. dead. back to the boot screen. we assumed it was our fault, obviously, because everything is your fault when you're four years deep into imposter syndrome. stack overflow? watchdog timeout? some cursed pointer bug? no. we checked the chip's reset reason and it said, very plainly, *brownout*. the laptop's USB port simply cannot push enough current to spin four motors. it's not a code problem. it's a "your power source is a usb port and you're asking it to fly" problem. the most honest error message i have ever received.

oh, and we turned off the safety feature that cuts the motors when the drone tilts too far, because the frame flexes so much it was triggering constantly and we couldn't get any testing done. the props are tiny plastic things that couldn't hurt a grape. we wrote it down as a "conscious safety trade-off" in the report, which is the academic way of saying "we knew, and we did it anyway."

so here's what we built, once you strip the ambition off it: a quadcopter that cannot fly. it's tethered to a laptop by a usb cable. it reboots if you give it too much throttle. its frame bends in the wind of its own propellers. no battery, no remote, no autonomy, no payload. C wanted to help win a war with this thing. it cannot lift itself off a desk.

and it was, hands down, the best thing we did all semester.

because for a few weeks i was 12 again, jailbreaking the ipad, building the shitty minecraft world, not optimizing anything, not monetizing anything, just trying to make a dumb flying robot fly because flying robots are cool. we built something that didn't matter. and it was enough.
